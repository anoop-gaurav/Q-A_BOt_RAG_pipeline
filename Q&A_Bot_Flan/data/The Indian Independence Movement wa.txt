The Indian Independence Movement was a prolonged struggle against British colonial rule, culminating in India’s independence in 1947. It was characterized by a combination of political activism, mass movements, and ideological diversity.

British rule in India began with the East India Company and later transitioned to direct control by the British Crown. Over time, economic exploitation, cultural interference, and political repression led to growing discontent among Indians.

The Indian National Congress, founded in 1885, became a major platform for political expression. Early leaders sought reforms through dialogue, but later leaders adopted more assertive strategies.

Mahatma Gandhi played a pivotal role by introducing non-violent resistance, or Satyagraha. Movements such as the Non-Cooperation Movement, Civil Disobedience Movement, and Quit India Movement mobilized millions of people.

Other leaders, such as Subhas Chandra Bose, advocated for armed struggle. The movement saw participation from various sections of society, including women and youth.

India achieved independence on August 15, 1947. However, the partition into India and Pakistan led to widespread violence and displacement. Despite this, the movement remains a powerful example of collective struggle and non-violent resistance.
